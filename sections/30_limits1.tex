\section{Limits}
\label{sec:limits}

	Limits are a precise mathematical language for talking about
	infinitely large numbers,
	infinitely small lengths,
	and procedures with an infinite number of steps.
	We use the shorthand ``$\lim$'' to write limit expressions.
	For example,
	the expression $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)$,
	is read ``the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ goes to infinity,''
	and describes what happens to $f(x)$ when $x$ gets larger and larger.


	\subsection{Example 1: Archimedes' approximation to $\pi$}
	% ALT. Archimedes' approximation for area of a circle

		We'll start by looking at a visual example of a math procedure
		% with infinite number of steps
		that was invented by Archimedes of Syracuse around 250 BCE.
		Archimedes wanted to calculate the area of a circle of radius $r=1$.
		Today we know the formula for the area of the circle is $A_{\circ} = \pi r^2$,
		so the area of a circle with radius $r=1$ is $\pi$.
		Try to place yourself in Archimedes's shoes (sandals?)
		and suppose that you don't know the formula.
		How can you find the area?

		Archimedes had the clever idea to approximate the circle
		as a regular polygon with $n$ sides inscribed inside the circle.
		Figure~\ref{fig:inscribed-hexagon-octagon-dodecagon}
		shows the hexagonal (6-sided),
		octagonal (8-sides),
		and dodecagonal (12-sided) approximations to the circle.

		\begin{figure}[htb]
			\centering
			\includegraphics[width=0.99\columnwidth]{figures/calculus/inscribed-polygons.pdf}%
			\vspace{-3mm}
			\caption{	Approximations to the area of a circle using a hexagon,
					an octagon, and a dodecagon inscribed inside a circle of radius $r$.}
			\label{fig:inscribed-hexagon-octagon-dodecagon}
		\end{figure}

		Archimedes split the area of the $n$-sided regular polygons into $2n$ triangular slices,
		like the one shown in Figure~\ref{fig:inscribed-hexagon-octagon-dodecagon}~(b).
		He then computed the area of each slice using the formula for the area of a triangle,
		and added up the areas of the $2n$ triangles
		to obtain the total area of the $n$-sided polygon.
		Let's denote by $A(n)$ the area approximation
		computed from a $n$-sided polygon.
		Looking at Figure~\ref{fig:inscribed-hexagon-octagon-dodecagon},
		we see the approximations to the area of the circle
		using six-sided and eight-sided polygons are underestimates for the total area.
		However,
		the polygon with $n=12$ is starting to look almost like a circle.
		We can use our imagination to see that the approximation $A(n) \approx A_{\circ}$
		will become more accurate as $n$ becomes larger and larger.
		Archimedes only managed to compute an approximation with a $96$-sided polygon,
		but thanks to computers we can push the approximation to much higher values of $n$.
		For example,
		using a $1000$-sided polygon gives us
		an approximation that is accurate to four decimals $A(1000) = 3.1415\ldots$.
		Using $n=10\,000$ we get an approximation to $\pi$
		that is accurate to six decimals $A(10000) = 3.141592\ldots$.
		See the computational notebook \href{https://bit.ly/calctut3}{\tt{bit.ly/calctut3}}
		for the details of these calculations.

		In the limit as $n$ goes to infinity,
		the approximation $A(n)$
		becomes \emph{exactly} equal to $\pi = 3.141592653589793\ldots$,
		which we can express as the limit expression $\lim_{n \to \infty} A(n) = \pi$.
		Note that $A(n) \neq \pi$ for any finite number $n$,
		no matter how large $n$ is.
		It is only in the limit as $n$ goes to infinity that the $n$-sided polygon approximation
		becomes exactly equal to a circle.

	that produces a useful approximation.


	\subsection{Example 2: Euler's number $e$}
	\label{introduction:eulers_number}

		Suppose you take out a loan with a 100\% interest rate.
		This is a very bad loan that nobody would agree to in the real world,
		but we'll use it for this example to make the math come out simpler.
		An interest rate of $100\%$ calculated yearly means at the end of one year,
		you'll owe the bank $(1+100\%) = (1+1)=2$ times the amount you borrowed initially.

		However,
		most banks don't calculate the interest only once per year.
		If the bank calculates the interest twice per year,
		during the first six months you'll have accrued $\frac{100\%}{2} = 50\%$ of interest,
		so you'll owe them $(1+50\%) = (1+\frac{1}{2}) = 1.5$ times the initial amount.
		Then during the second six months,
		the amount owed will grow by an additional $(1+50\%) = (1+\frac{1}{2}) = 1.5$,
		so at the end of the year,
		you'll owe them $(1+\frac{1}{2})(1+\frac{1}{2}) = 2.25$.

		If the bank computes the interest three times per year,
		the amount owed after one year is $(1+\frac{1}{3})(1+\frac{1}{3})(1+\frac{1}{3}) = 2.370$.
		If they compute the interest four times per year (quarterly),
		then you'll owe $(1+\frac{1}{4})(1+\frac{1}{4})(1+\frac{1}{4})(1+\frac{1}{4})  = 2.441$.
		Note the amount owed after one year keeps changing,
		as the compounding is performed more frequently.
		In general,
		when the compounding is performed $n$ times per year,
		the amount owed at the end of one year will be
		\[
			\underbrace{
			\left(1 + \tfrac{1}{n} \right)
			\left(1 + \tfrac{1}{n} \right)
			\cdots
			\left(1 + \tfrac{1}{n} \right)
			}_{n \text{ times}}
			= 
			\left(1 + \tfrac{1}{n} \right)^{\!n}.
		\]
		With monthly compounding ($n=12$),
		the amount owed will be $(1 + \tfrac{1}{12})^{12} = 2.613$
		at the end of one year.
		With daily compounding,
		the amount would be $(1 + \tfrac{1}{365})^{365} = 2.715$.
		If computing the interest $n=1000$ times per year,
		the amount will be $(1 + \tfrac{1}{1000})^{1000} = 2.717$.
		The amount owed keeps increasing,
		but it seems to ``stabilize'' around the value $2.71$.

		What happens if we perform the compounding even more frequently?
		Specifically,
		we want to know what happens if the interest is compounded \emph{infinitely often}.
		The infinitely-often calculation corresponds
		to computing the \emph{limit} of expression  $(1 + \frac{1}{n})^n$,
		as $n$ goes to infinity,
		which is written as follows using math notation:
		\[
			\lim_{n\to \infty} \left( 1 + \tfrac{1}{n}\right)^{\!n}
				\;\; = \;\; e
				\;\;= \;\; 2.718281828\ldots.
		\]
		This limit expression \emph{converges} to the value $e = 2.71828\ldots$,
		which is known as \emph{Euler's number}.
		If we borrow $\$1000$,
		we'll owe $\$1000e = \$2718.28$ at the end of one~year.		
		% The number $e$ describes the limit of the annual growth rate of a loan
		% compound interest calculation for an annual interest rate of $100\%$
		% with compounding is performed infinitely often.


	We defined the number $\pi$ as the limit $\lim_{n \to \infty} A(n)$
	and the number $e$ as the limit $\lim_{n\to \infty} \big( 1 + \tfrac{1}{n}\big)^{\!n}$.
	In both cases,
	we define some computational procedure with $n$ steps,
	then consider what happens as $n$ goes to infinity.
	The limit expression $\lim_{n \to \infty}$ doesn't describe any particular number $n$,
	but the \emph{process} of plugging in larger and larger values of $n$.



	\subsection{Limits at infinity}
	\label{limits:limits_to_infinity}

		We can use a limit expressions to describe
		what happens to a function when its input variable tends to infinity.
		Does $f(x)$ approach a finite number,
		or does it keep growing?
		We say the function $f(x)$ \emph{converges} to $L$
		if the function approaches the value $L$ for large values of $x$:
		\[
			\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)  =  L.
		\]
		In words,
		this math expression states that ``The limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ goes to infinity is $L$.''
		See Figure~\ref{fig:limit-at-infinity-graph} for an illustration.

		\begin{figure}[htb]
			\centering
			\includegraphics[width=0.7\columnwidth]{figures/calculus/limit-at-infinity-graph.png}
			\vspace{-3mm}
			\caption{	The function $f(x)$ oscillates up and down initially,
					but then it converges (settles down) close to the value $L$ for large values of $x$.}
			\label{fig:limit-at-infinity-graph}
		\end{figure}


		\subsubsection{Example 3}

			Consider the limit of the function $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$ as $x$ goes to infinity,
			which is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:limits_examples}~(a):
			\[
				\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)
					= 	\lim_{x \to \infty} \tfrac{1}{x}
					= 	0.
			\]
			The function $\tfrac{1}{x}$ never \emph{actually} reaches zero,
			so it would be wrong to write $f(x)=0$ for any $x \in \mathbb{R}$.
			However,
			the expression $\frac{1}{x}$ gets closer and closer to $0$ as $x$ goes to infinity.
			Limits are useful because they allow us to describe this tendency.

	\subsection{Limit formulas}

		The limits of the sum, difference, product, and quotient of two functions
		are computed as follows:
		% is equal to the corresponding operation of the limits of the two functions:
		\begin{align*}	
			\lim_{x \to \infty} (f(x) + g(x)) 	& =  \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) + \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x), 	\\
			\lim_{x \to \infty} (f(x) - g(x)) 	& =  \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) - \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x), 	\\
			\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)g(x) 		& =   \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x), \\
			\lim_{x \to \infty} (f(x) / g(x)) & =  \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) \, / \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x) \, .
			% \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} & =  \frac{ \displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) } { \displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x)} \, .
		\end{align*}

		\noindent
		In words,
		these formulas tell us we
		can bring the limit calculations ``inside'' basic arithmetic operations.


		\subsubsection{Example 4}

			Calculate $\lim_{x\to \infty} \frac{2x+1}{x}\,$.
			We're given the function $g(x)=\frac{2x+1}{x}$
			and must determine what the function looks like for very large values of $x$.
			We can rewrite the function as $\frac{2x+1}{x}=2+\frac{1}{x}$
			then apply the sum formula for limits:
			\[
				\lim_{x\to \infty}\!\! \tfrac{2x+1}{x} 
					= \lim_{x\to \infty}\!\Big( 2 + \tfrac{1}{x} \Big)
					= \lim_{x\to \infty} 2 + \!\lim_{x\to \infty} \tfrac{1}{x} 
					= 2 + 0 = 2.
			\]
			As $x$ goes to infinity,
			$\frac{1}{x}$ goes to $0$,
			so the second term vanishes,
			leaving only the $2$.
			See Figure~\ref{fig:limits_examples}~(b) for an illustration.



	\subsection{Limits at a point}
	\label{limits:limits_at_a_point}

		We also use limit expressions to describe what happens
		when a function approaches some number $x=a$.
		The limit expression $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$
		describes the behaviour of the function $f(x)$
		as $x$ approaches $a$ \emph{from the right}.
		To compute this limit,
		we imagine a small positive number $\delta > 0$ (the Greek letter \emph{delta})
		that gets smaller and smaller,
		and compute the following equivalent limit expression
		\[
			\lim_{x\to a^+} f(x) = \lim_{\delta \to 0} f(a + \delta).
		\]
		This limit expression describes the value of the function $f$
		for inputs like $a+0.1$, $a+0.01$, $a+0.001$, $a+0.0001$, etc.
		In other words,
		the input $x$ gets closer and closer to $a$ (from the right)
		without ever touching $x=a$.

		For example,
		the limit $\lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x)$,
		read ``the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ goes to zero,''
		asks us to evaluate the function~$f$ for inputs like
		$x=0.1$, $x=0.01$, $x=0.001$, $x=0.0001$, etc.
		The outputs of $f(x) =~\frac{1}{x}$ get larger and larger
		as $x$ takes on smaller and smaller values,
		so the limit goes to infinity:
		$\lim_{x\to 0^+} \tfrac{1}{x}
			= \lim_{\delta \to 0} \tfrac{1}{0^+\delta}
			= \lim_{\delta \to 0} \tfrac{1}{\delta} = \infty$.	
		In words,
		the function $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$ ``blows up'' to infinity as $x$ goes to $0^+$,
		as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:limits_examples}~(a).

		The limit of $f(x)$ when $x$ approaches $a$ \emph{from the left}
		is defined analogously,
		$\lim_{x\to a^-} f(x)  = \lim_{\delta \to 0^+} f(a - \delta)$.
		For example,
		the limit of $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$
		as $x$ approaches zero from the left
		is $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty$.
		%
		If both limits from the left and from the right at $x=a$
		exist and are equal to each other,
		we can drop the $^+$ and $^-$ annotations,
		and talk about the limit as $x\to a$:
		\[
			\lim_{x\to a} f(x) =  \lim_{x\to a^+} f(x) =  \lim_{x\to a^-} f(x).
		\]
		For the two-sided limit of a function to exist at a point,
		both the limit from the left and the limit from the right must converge to the same number.
		We saw this  is not the case for the function $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$ at $x=0$,
		so the two-sided limit $\lim_{x\to 0} f(x)$ doesn't exist.






	\subsection{Continuity}
	\label{limits:continuity}

		If the function $f(x)$ satisfies $f(a) = \lim_{x\to a} f(x)$,
		we say the function $f(x)$ is \emph{continuous} at $x=a$.
		Geometrically,
		the graph of the continuous function at $x=a$ is a smooth curve
		that doesn't have a hole or a jump at $x=a$.
		When a function is continuous,
		we can draw its graph using a single pen stroke
		without lifting the pen.
		The two-sided limit $\lim_{x\to a}$ requires both the left and the right limit to exist and to be equal.
		Thus,
		the definition of continuity implies the equalities $\lim_{x \to a^-}  f(x) =  f(a)$
		and $f(a) = \lim_{x \to a^+}  f(x)$,
		which correspond to the idea of ``not lifting the pen'' when drawing the graph of $f(x)$ at $x=a$.

		In contrast,
		functions that are not defined at a point,
		functions that blow up to infinity,
		and functions that make sudden jumps are not continuous.
		For example,
		the function $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$
		is discontinuous at $x=0$,
		since $\frac{1}{x}$ is not defined when $x=0$.
		Also,
		the limits $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = \infty$ and $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty$
		are not the same,
		so the two-sided limit $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x}$ doesn't exist.


		\subsubsection{Example 5}
			
			Let's investigate the behaviour of the function $g(x)=\frac{2x+1}{x}$ near the value $x=1$.
			Is $g(x)$ continuous at $x=1$?
			First we evaluate the function $g(x)$ for the input $x=1$,
			which gives us $g(1) = \frac{2(1)+1}{1} = \frac{3}{1}=3$.
			Nothing tricky going on here---we just plug the number $1$ into the formula for $g(x)$ to get the value of the function.

			Next we compute the limit from the right at $x=1$
			which is:
			\[
				\lim_{x\to 1^+}\! g(x)
					= \!\!\lim_{x\to 1^+}\!\! \tfrac{2x+1}{x}
					= \lim_{\delta \to 0}\! \tfrac{2(1+\delta)+1}{1+\delta}
					= \lim_{\delta \to 0}\! \tfrac{2+2\delta+1}{1+\delta}
					= \tfrac{3}{1}
					= 3.
			\]
			The $\delta$ terms in the numerator and denominator go to zero.

			We evaluate the limit from the left using similar steps:
			$\lim_{x\to 1^-} g(x) = \lim_{x\to 1^-} \frac{2x+1}{x} = \lim_{\delta \to 0} \tfrac{2(1-\delta)+1}{1-\delta} = 3$.
			Since the limits from the left and the right are equal,
			$\lim_{x\to 1^+} g(x) = \lim_{x\to 1^-} g(x)$,
			we say that the two-sided limit exists $\lim_{x\to 1} g(x) = 3$.
			Since $\lim_{x\to 1} g(x) = 3 = g(1)$,
			the function $g(x)$ is continuous at $x=1$.

			Intuitively,
			the graph of the function $g(x)$ at $x=1$ looks like a smooth curve.
			Imagine the pen stroke drawing the graph at $x=1$:
			it comes in from the left at height $\lim_{x\to 1^-} g(x) =~3$,
			goes through $g(1) = 3$,
			then leaves to the right at the same height $\lim_{x\to 1^+} g(x) = 3$.
			The function $g(x)=\frac{2x+1}{x}$ is continuous everywhere except at $x=0$.




	\subsection{Computing limits using SymPy}
	
		We can use SymPy to compute limit expressions,
		which allows us to check the answers we obtain using pen-and-paper limit calculations.
		We start by importing the \tt{sympy} module under the alias \tt{sp}
		and defining the symbolic variable $\tt{n}$,
		which we can then use to write math expressions.

		\begin{codeblock}[def-n-symbol]
		>>> import sympy as sp
		>>> n = sp.symbols("n")
		\end{codeblock}

		We can use the SymPy function \tt{sp.limit(<expr>,var,value)}
		to evaluate a limit $\lim_{\tt{var} \to \tt{value}} \tt{<expr>}$.
		%	as the variable \tt{var} approaches \tt{value}.
		% ALT
		%	which evaluates the expression \tt{expr},
		%	as the variable \tt{var} approaches \tt{value}.
		For limits to infinity,
		we use the symbol \texttt{sp.oo} (two lowercase \texttt{o}s),
		which kind of looks like the infinity symbol $\infty$.

		% We can use SymPy to check the limit calculation we saw earlier in this section.
		% EXAMPLE  e
		Euler's number is defined as the limit
		$e \eqdef \lim_{n\to \infty} \left( 1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^{n}$.
		To compute this limit using SymPy,
		we call \tt{sp.limit} on the expression \tt{(1+1/n)**n}
		as $\tt{n}$ goes to infinity $\infty = \texttt{sp.oo}$:

		\begin{codeblock}[sympy-e-from-limit]
		>>> sp.limit((1+1/n)**n, n, sp.oo)
		E
		>>> sp.limit((1+1/n)**n, n, sp.oo).evalf(40)
		2.718281828459045235360287471352662497757
		\end{codeblock}


		\noindent
		The result of \tt{sp.limit} is the exact value $e$
		which is represented symbolically as \tt{E}.
		We then call the method \tt{.evalf(40)} 
		to obtain an approximation to $e$ that is accurate to 40 decimals.

		% EXAMPLE 1/x to \infty
		Let's now compute the limit $\lim_{x \to \infty} \tfrac{1}{x}$.
		We must first define the symbol \tt{x}
		so that we can write the expression $\tt{1/x} = \frac{1}{x}$,
		which we then evaluate using the function \tt{sp.limit}:

		\begin{codeblock}[]
		>>> x = sp.symbols("x")
		>>> sp.limit(1/x, x, sp.oo)
		0
		\end{codeblock}

		\noindent
		This result confirms the answer $\lim_{x \to \infty} \tfrac{1}{x}=0$
		that we obtained earlier in Example~3.


		% EXAMPLE 1/x left and right
		To evaluate the limit from the right or the left at a point,
		we must provide a fourth argument,
		either \tt{"+"} or \tt{"-"},
		when calling the function \tt{sp.limit}.
		We can evaluate the limits $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \tfrac{1}{x}$
		and $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \tfrac{1}{x}$ as follows:

		\begin{codeblock}[]
		>>> sp.limit(1/x, x, 0, "+")
		oo
		>>> sp.limit(1/x, x, 0, "-")
		-oo
		\end{codeblock}
		
		\noindent
		We confirm the limits $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \tfrac{1}{x} = \infty$
		and $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \tfrac{1}{x} = -\infty$
		that we calculated earlier.
		See Figure~\ref{fig:limits_examples}~(a) for an illustration.


		% EXAMPLE (2x+1)/x at infinity
		Let's now revisit Example~4,
		and compute the limit of the function $g(x) = \frac{2x+1}{x}$ as $x$ goes to infinity:

		\begin{codeblock}[]
		>>> sp.limit((2*x+1)/x, x, sp.oo)
		2
		\end{codeblock}

		This confirms the result illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:limits_examples}~(b).


		% EXAMPLE (2x+1)/x at x=1
		We can also confirm the continuity calculations
		for the function $g(x) = \frac{2x+1}{x}$ at $x=1$,
		which we saw in Example~5.
		We can evaluate the limit at $x=1$ from the left $\lim_{x \to 1^-} g(x)$,
		the value of the function at $x=1$,
		and limit from the right $\lim_{x \to 1^+} g(x)$
		using the following code:

		\begin{codeblock}[]
		>>> sp.limit((2*x+1)/x, x, 1, "-")
		3
		>>> ((2*x+1)/x).subs({x:1})  # = g(1)
		3
		>>> sp.limit((2*x+1)/x, x, 1, "+")
		3
		\end{codeblock}
		
		\noindent
		The fact that all three expressions % exist and
		are equal to each other,
		\[
			\lim_{x \to 1^-} g(x) = g(1) = \lim_{x \to 1^+} g(x),
		\]
		tells us that the function $g(x)=\frac{2x+1}{x}$ is continuous at $x=1$.

	\subsection{Applications of limits}

		Limits are important because they are used in the formal definitions of derivatives, integrals, and series:

		\begin{itemize}

			\item The derivative function $f^{\prime\!}(x)$
				describes the rate of change of the function $f(x)$ at $x$.
				In Section~\ref{sec:derivatives},
				we'll calculate derivatives by evaluating limits of the form $\lim_{\delta \to 0}$.

			\item The integral $\int_a^b f(x) \, dx$
				describes the area under the graph of the function $f(x)$ between $x=a$ and $x=b$.
				In Section~\ref{sec:integrals},
				we'll learn how to compute integrals by splitting up areas into $n$ rectangles,
				then taking the limit $\lim_{n \to \infty}$.

			\item The series $\sum_{k=1}^n a_k$ describes
				the sum of the first $n$ terms in the sequence $a_k$.
				In Section~\ref{sec:sequences_and_series},
				we'll learn how to compute infinite series by evaluating the limit
				$\lim_{n \to \infty}\sum_{k=1}^n a_k$.

		\end{itemize}

		\noindent
		In the remainder of this tutorial,
		we'll use limits to evaluate all kinds of derivatives, integrals, and series expressions.
		In each of these domains,
		\textbf{limit expressions will help us} make precise statements
		that \textbf{describe calculus procedures} that involve infinitely short lengths
		and an infinite number of steps.
